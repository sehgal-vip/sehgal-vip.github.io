Three Product Thinking Frameworks That Changed How I Build

After years of building products, I’ve found that the right mental model at the right time can unlock breakthroughs. Here are three frameworks that fundamentally changed how I approach product development.

1. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

The insight: People don’t buy products. They hire them to do a job.

When someone buys a drill, they don’t want a drill—they want a hole. When they download a meditation app, they don’t want an app—they want calm.

Applying JTBD

Ask: “What job is the customer hiring my product to do?”

The answer reveals:

  • Competitors you didn’t consider (a milkshake competes with bananas and boredom)
  • Features that actually matter
  • Marketing messages that resonate

2. The Kano Model

The insight: Not all features create satisfaction the same way.

Features fall into categories:

  1. Basic (Must-haves): Expected. Absence causes frustration; presence is unnoticed.
  2. Performance (One-dimensional): More is better. Directly proportional satisfaction.
  3. Delighters (Exciters): Unexpected. Create disproportionate joy.

Applying Kano

  • Ensure basics are solid (table stakes)
  • Compete on performance features
  • Invest in strategic delighters

Over time, delighters become expectations. Stay ahead.

3. Opportunity Solution Trees

The insight: Connect outcomes to opportunities to solutions.

Created by Teresa Torres, this framework prevents jumping to solutions:

Outcome (What we want to achieve)
    ├── Opportunity 1 (Customer need/pain)
    │   ├── Solution A
    │   └── Solution B
    └── Opportunity 2
        └── Solution C

Applying OST

  1. Start with a clear outcome metric
  2. Map customer opportunities (needs, pains, desires)
  3. Generate multiple solutions per opportunity
  4. Test and learn

This prevents “feature factory” mode and ensures work connects to outcomes.

Bringing It Together

I use these frameworks in sequence:

  1. JTBD to understand what job customers hire the product for
  2. Kano to categorize and prioritize features
  3. OST to systematically explore and validate solutions

They’re not mutually exclusive—they’re complementary lenses for the same problem: building things people actually want.
